A pretrained language model, which we call pi-ref, is trained by maximizing the likelihood of human-written text. That objective produces fluent language, but fluency is not the same as helpfulness, harmlessness, or honesty. We need a way to steer pi-ref toward outputs humans actually prefer. That is the alignment problem. The solution RLHF, which stands for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, works in three stages. Stage one: collect preference data. Given a prompt x, sample two responses y-A and y-B from the model, show them to a human labeler, and record which they prefer. Stage two: train a reward model, which we call R of x comma y. R is a neural network, usually initialized from pi-ref itself, fine-tuned to assign a scalar score to any prompt-response pair so that preferred responses receive higher scores. Concretely, R is trained with a Bradley-Terry loss: minimize the negative log probability that R of x comma y-A is greater than R of x comma y-B, given that y-A was preferred. Stage three: use reinforcement learning to fine-tune a new policy pi, meaning our updated language model, to maximize the expected reward E of R of x comma y under pi. The algorithm most commonly used is PPO, proximal policy optimization, which clips policy update ratios to keep updates stable. But here is the failure mode called reward hacking. If we maximize R unconstrained, pi learns to produce outputs that fool R into giving high scores, outputs that are repetitive, verbose, or semantically degenerate, because R is only an approximation of true human preference. To prevent this, we add a KL divergence penalty. KL of pi given pi-ref measures how far the new policy pi has drifted from the reference pi-ref in bits. The full RLHF objective is J of pi equals E over prompts x and responses y drawn from pi of the quantity R of x comma y minus beta times KL of pi given pi-ref. Beta is a non-negative scalar hyperparameter. When beta is near zero, the model is free to chase high reward and risks hacking. When beta is large, the model stays close to pi-ref and barely changes. In practice beta is tuned between 0.01 and 0.5.
